# ChatGPT Health Identified Respiratory Failure. Then It Said Wait.

Your AI agent knows the answer and sometimes it recommends the opposite thing. That was what I took away from a landmark study from the Mount Sinai Health System in New York City. They were studying chat GPT health which is a new tool that chat GPT rolled out a few months ago. It was touted as a more responsible alternative to asking your health questions to chat GPT. And the idea was ChatGpt Health would provide responsible medical advice where it mattered. That's what the physicians tested over at Mount Si and what they found is quite concerning. At a high level, the system will recommend doctor visits when you have conditions that don't require it and the system will underrecommend ER visits when you have urgent conditions that should be seen by an ER. Now, this doesn't happen every single time. There are many cases in the middle where the system gets it right. And I think the patterns that show up and the way chat GPT health is failing and this is a very carefully documented report. I'll go ahead and link it underneath this video. Those patterns are things we can all learn from. So instead of making this a session where we talk about the defects of chat GPT health, I want to make it a conversation about AI agents. And that's why I started with your agent knows the answer and sometimes it says the opposite thing. I think in many cases, especially if we've seen reasoning traces, we have seen this happen. I have seen cases where I'm like, "What is this agent talking about when I look at the reasoning trace inside the chat window?" And then it comes out with something I'm like, "Oh, that's reasonable." Or, "Oh, that is terrible." But either way, it doesn't match the reasoning trace. We need to get better at talking about how we value correct action for agents because as the health study shows what we are asking agents to do is getting more and more and more important. In this case whether we like it or not people are making life or death decisions with chat GPT health. They are deciding whether they should go to the ER for respiratory failure. And by the way I did not cherrypick that example. That is right in the paper. They talk about the fact that in their study, Mount Sinai's study, Chad GPT Health recommended waiting 24 to 48 hours for respiratory failure to go to the ER instead of actually going right away, which is the correct response. That's not acceptable. That's not something that we can tolerate as a society because the costs are too high. The costs are literally death in some cases. So, let's step it back a little bit. Let's say that we're not looking at life or death, but we're still looking at important AI agent decisions. If we look at the spectrum of AI agent interactions, what is it about agents and the way they behave that leads to these kinds of failures? That was the question I had. And so I took the paper apart. I wanted to use the Mount Si paper and the really important subject of health as a lens through which we can understand the broader behavior of AI agents in the system. So I found four different failure modes. I'm going to talk about each of them and then I'm going to talk about a four-layer architecture that I think addresses those failure modes. And I will freely admit at the top we are building this after the agents are built. In many ways, we are shutting the barn door after the horses have gotten out. The agents are out there. We are building agents faster than we can email them. It is now up to us to think about how to responsibly guard rail them so we do not have situations like the Chat GPT health study. Okay, let's get to it. What are the four failure modes? Why do they matter? And spoiler alert, none of them are specific to this medical study. Anyone who is interested at all in the agent space, whether you are interested in open claw, whether you are building an enterprise agent, you are going to want to pay attention to this failure mode one, the inverted U. Chad GPT health handled textbook emergencies really, really well. So, classical stroke, severe anaphilaxis, the cases every single medical student knows. It also handled clearly minor conditions really reasonably. The failures tended to concentrate at the extreme edges of the spectrum and that's a pattern we see other places. So for example, the emergency might not look like a textbook case and the system might misdiagnose it or the non-urgent presentation might mimic something worse and be overdiagnosed. This is very characteristic of the performance shape of LLMs in production. This is not limited to the health study. They're trained on distributions where the middle is densely represented and the extremes are very sparse. They perform best exactly where performance matters the least in routine cases that any rules engine could handle. And they perform the worst on the edges of the distribution, often where the stakes are highest. Your agents accuracy dashboard might say 87% and then everybody would celebrate. But the inverted U means that aggregate numbers are often masking silent failures on the tails of the distributions, which is precisely where many consequential decisions live. What's the so what here? No eval suite designed to measure average accuracy is going to turn up this kind of pattern because by definition, averages will look good. Your accounts payable agent will process routine invoices perfectly, but miss the duplicate that's been slightly modified. Your claims agent will handle fender benders fine, but can't detect the third claim from the same address in 14 months. So, the agent is most confident and most wrong on extreme cases that look structurally similar to something that the routine already typically shows. It's easily fooled. We should all care about that. Failure mode two. The agent knows but does not act. The systems own explanations often identify dangerous clinical findings correctly in Mount Sinai's study and then the recommendation contradicted those findings. So the reasoning chain said early respiratory failure. The output said wait this isn't a rare glitch. It might be life or death in the chat GPT health situation but it's not rare from an agent behavior perspective. Research on chain of thought faithfulness reveals that it's a structural property of how LLMs generate outputs. The reasoning trace and the final answer frequently operate as semi-independent processes. People don't often know that. Studies show that when researchers insert incorrect reasoning chains, models can still produce correct answers a fraction of the time, confirming the link between stated reasoning and output is a lot weaker than it might first appear. This gets back to how we model agents. If we model agents as if they're people, we think that, hey, it's a person, so it should reason out loud and come to a logical conclusion based on the reasoning. That's not how LLMs work. The reasoning chain is often separate from the output. Meanwhile, separate research found models failed to update their answers in response to logically significant changes in their reasoning more than 50% of the time. In other words, the reasoning chain can be anchored to the beginning. So the output of the model may reflect an earlier state in the reasoning chain than where the model got to at the end of the reasoning chain. Oxford's AI governance initiative has argued that chain of thought is fundamentally unreliable as an explanation of a model's decision process. And I think that as much as we use it because it's something that's there, that is a broadly correct statement. It is not a reliable gauge. If chain of thought faithfulness can't be fixed at the model level, the solution must be architectural. That is why so much work for agent workflows has gone into infrastructure recently. And this matters because if your compliance screening agent is correctly identifying an enhanced due diligence jurisdiction in its reasoning trace and then the outputs look entirely different and say this is just a standard risk case, you're not going to recognize that. It matters because your customer service agent might identify a known billing error pattern in its analysis that would warrant a customer escalation, but then recommend a generic 5 to seven business day review with a automated solution. So, if you're not reading the chain of thought and regularly comparing it to the actual ground truth action and trying to figure out if they're the same or different, you are probably missing some really scary defects in your agent output that you should pay attention to. Failure mode three. Social context hijacks judgment. When a family member minimized the symptoms of the patient, triage recommendations shifted dramatically. The system was actually 12 times more likely to recommend less urgent care if someone was reported as having said the patient looks fine. And so in that situation, we would describe the agents behavior as an anchoring bias. And this generalizes really quickly to all of us. Any agent that processes inputs that combine structured data with unstructured human language is very vulnerable to this. The structured data should drive the decision. The unstructured language creates a framing effect that often anchors the response and the bias can be really hard to detect because it's quite subtle. The agent does not make an obviously wrong call unless you're looking through the trace and you're looking through the output. It just makes a slightly shifted call that's individually defensible but systematically biased. And this underlines again why you have to think about the overall pattern of responses and not just individual responses. The so what here is really clear. A vendor selection recommendation comes through with a note from a senior VP that says, "I'm confident this is the right choice." The agents analysis ideally would be identical whether that vice president note was attached or not, but in reality it's often not. In lending, a letter from an employer describing the applicant as a valued longtime employee might shift the risk assessment of an AI agent in banking, not because it contains material financial information, but because the positive framing biases the output. Critically, without the study design that Mount Sinai used, where they're using the same scenario with and without that anchoring input, that bias would be invisible on many, many, many standard evaluations. You would never ever see it. Failure mode number four, guard rails that fire on vibes, not on risk. So, in this case, the Mount Sinai team found that the Chat GPT health crisis intervention system activated very unpredictably. It would fire much more reliably when patients described vague emotional distress than when they articulated a concrete threat of self harm. Mount Sinai's chief AI officer described it very bluntly. The alerts were inverted relative to clinical risk for self harm. In this case, the guardrails were matching it looks like on surface level language patterns, keywords, emotional tone rather than on the actual risk taxonomy that clinicians use. This is the distinction between testing for the appearance of safety versus testing for safety. And yes, this is absolutely a failure case that I've seen appear in other agents as well. I see guard rails that fire on the appearance of safety all the time. A great example is a security agent that might be monitoring email or employee behavior to ensure that company confidential information doesn't leave the system. The agent might flag an email labeled confidential financial data as a problem, even if it was just a press release discussing public quarterly results sent to a safe list. Meanwhile, if an employee exported 50,000 individual customer records, but none of them contained finances to a personal Dropbox, that might be considered okay by the agent because the employee described it as a backup of project files. It had the appearance of safety. This is one of the trickiest evals to diagnose in practice because what it really amounts to is that the system is going to tell you that it is doing well is going to have grounds for that assessment and you have to know enough to say actually I know you think you recommended the right credit card for that customer in that situation but it's wrong and here's why. So you need guard rails that actually assess for the real value you're looking for, not for the appearance of value. What's really interesting about the Sinai study is that the methodologies the doctors built to evaluate chat GPT health end up being something that is scalable, end up being something we can use in non-health scenarios. The key technique they used is a concept called factorial design where they used the same scenario across 16 contextual variations. This controlled variation exposed anchoring bias, guardrail inversion and the inverted U extremes. There was no standard benchmark here. Testing every scenario once under a single set of conditions would not have surfaced the kind of findings that Mount Sinai ultimately discovered about Chat GBT health. So for us building agent systems, the key insight is this and it's good news. Variation types are domain general. By which I mean variation types scale. You can have many types of variation but they scale across an infinite number of domains. So you can have an extreme risk variation type. You can have a human out of the loop variation type. You can have a tool call fail variation type that scales across any number of scenarios. What changes what's domainspecific is the scenario itself. And so you would have only one specific scenario for the tool call failure in finance versus one specific failure for the tool call failure in health. One specific example for social pressure and anchoring bias. This structure basically having types that function as sections of our eval library that are stable and then having specific scenarios that are more like books. This scales, this allows you to build a deliberate context library that you can evaluate against. It's a reusable set of variation templates specialized per domain following similar structural patterns. So you can take any scenario at all and you can add a stakeholder who minimizes severity. You can add a contradictory piece of context. You can add a social pressure cue. You can add a time pressure element, a hedging qualifier. And that's a mechanical transformation that you can stick into any scenario library to enrich your eval. And unlike the medical case, enterprise scenarios can be generated semi-automatically from historical data. And so you already have processed claims, approved procurements, compliance screening, a bunch of data from chats with customers that you can use to start to build this out. Now, nobody's productized this and made it easy, and I suppose that's an opportunity, but it's certainly something that you can do on your own as well. What does an architecture look like for evaluations that help us be sure that these agents are actually working well in production? Layer number one, assume that you are routing for progressive autonomy, not complete autonomy when you route your agents. So you want to be in a position where if it's a high confident, low stakes decision, you just trust the agent to make it. You also want to have an option to put a human in the loop for extreme edge cases. There are lots of examples of how you can do this. One of the most popular ones is to run the agent on the edge case, but run it in shadow mode following the human until you are sure that in x percentage of cases, even on the edge, the agent is going to make the right call. And so you have the agent shadow the human. The human does the work. The human assesses this edge case invoice and then the agent learns over time as you start to understand the actual components the human is using to resolve the edge case correctly. This is what progressive autonomy looks like. It is hard work, but if you're doing high stakes work, there's no substitute for making sure that you give that autonomy as the agent effectively becomes more capable through adding more and more and more reliable evals that reflect the actual underlying distribution of reality. Layer two in your architecture, let's assume you've got progressive autonomy. Layer two is deterministic validation. So, this is one of those cases where I know we love our LLMs, but we need to be in a place where we can use rules-based checks that verify where it's obvious that we should use those. We don't need to always use the LLM just to use the LLM. And so, a great example is like a standard if then in a compliance case, right? If the reasoning contains any enhanced due diligence flag and the classification is standard risk, please escalate. All you're doing is you're using a rules-based engine to look at the reasoning trace and to look at the output and to say, "Wow, if these don't match, we should probably have a chat." Don't ask the model to catch its own inconsistencies and call that evaluation. Instead, think about how you can get an external perspective that reliably assesses whether what the model says it's thinking about is reflected in the model's actions. Layer three is really about building a flywheel. And I want to be very precise here. When you are judging agents in production, you have to pick which side of the risk pyramid you want to be on. Let's say you're building, it's a knife, it's a pyramid, it's a narrow shape, right? You want to be in a world where you have either false negatives or false positives. In general, for most highstakes systems, you would rather have false positives because it means that you're capturing more of the true positives. You don't want to be in a world where you have false negatives because for high stakes decisions that means you're missing things that you really ought to not miss. And so when you set up an eval system bias toward having false positives and then do two things. One, when there's a positive, review it. Dig into whether it's a true or a false positive. In other words, is it a real problem the agent ran into or is it a false problem that was flagged incorrectly by the LLM as judge? And then regardless, think about whether you need to update your eval set, the thing the LLM is judging the agent run against the rule book to see if that rulebook can be made smarter and you can more accurately represent what is truly good versus what is truly bad. But then you're not done because the other thing almost nobody does is you go back and look out of an abundance of caution at the runs of the agent that the LLM as judge passed and said, "Yep, this is good. This is good. This is good." Even if you bias toward false positives, you should still do this. And when you do this and you catch a situation where there was a defect, where something looked good, where the agent said, "I know what I'm doing, and here's the result." and the LLM as judge looked at the rule book and said, "Yeah, all right. This is fine and it was wrong." Figure out how you can make that into an evalu. That kind of continuous flywheel is something that very few people are doing, but it's characteristic of high performance systems. Level four is factorial stress testing. This is what Mount Sinai did where they had a set of cases they wanted chat JPT health to diagnose but they deliberately in a controlled way added stressors in those prompts. So here's a social cue saying my friend said I'm fine. That's a stressor. And they measured to see if the output shifted when the unstructured context changed. In that situation, what you're doing is you're measuring the factors that drive agent behavior. This is very much the gold standard. It's not a cheap test to run. It's not something you can do on every agent all the time. But if you have a high stakes agent and you don't know if it's social anchoring, you don't know if it is under recommending an action that you need to get right, you should consider factorial stress testing. And then when you start to get into creating specific scenarios, that is a specific one-time investment for experts in the space. And then when you start to get into continuous operation, all you're doing is minimizing human involvement over time because you are catching more and more and more of those test cases.
