# 90% of People Fail at Vibe Coding. Here's the Actual Reason: You're Skipping the Hard Part.

Something clicked in the last couple of weeks and the word I keep reaching for is playfulness with AI, which is probably not what you expect because the AI discourse is super loud right now and most of it to be honest is ominous. Jobs are disappearing, deep fakes, misinformation, existential risk, think pieces that are anxious, comment sections get angry. I'm not here to dismiss that. The concerns are real and happening in parallel that we miss. It's quieter. It's a little bit odd. And I want to talk about that instead today. So, vibe coding, which is using AI to build software with natural language, that's been possible since the earlier part of 2025. But for most of the year, it was still work. You had to fight the tools. You had to babysit the AI through the confusion. You had to debug weird failures that made no sense. You had to learn about databases and backend. The friction was high enough that you had to be serious about what you were building. You wouldn't want to waste that effort on something frivolous. What's shifted in the last couple of weeks is not just the tools getting better, but the threshold of effort has dropped so low that it's moved from the category of work into the category of play. And that is actually the secret reason why most people are failing at this. They're still treating it like a job or a system to be mastered, rather than a playground to be explored. When you're playing, you're not worried about the 'hard part'—the architecture, the scaling, the perfect code. You're just following a vibe. You're building something because it's funny, or because you're curious, or because you just want to see if it's possible. This 'vibe coding' is a renaissance of individual creativity. We're seeing people build apps in a weekend that used to take teams of engineers months. But they're not doing it by following a roadmap. They're doing it by following their intuition and playing with the AI as a creative partner. The 'hard part' that people are skipping isn't the coding—it's the specification. It's knowing exactly what you want and being able to describe it. But when you play, you're constantly refining that specification as you see the results. You're in a tight feedback loop of 'what if I did this?' and 'oh, that's cool, now let's try that.' This is the actual reason people fail: they try to be engineers instead of being players. They're skipping the fun, experimental part and trying to jump straight to the 'serious' product. But the best software of this new era is being built by people who are just having fun. They don't necessarily have growth metrics. They just have the pleasure of building their own tools and following the ideas where they lead. The exciting thing to me is that the tools exist now that make that degree of playfulness possible. And the internet truly becomes your playground. And frankly, if that's you, I'd love to see what you've built.
