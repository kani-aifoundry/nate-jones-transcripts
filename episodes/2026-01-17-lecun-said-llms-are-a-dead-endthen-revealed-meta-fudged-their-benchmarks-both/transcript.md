# LeCun Said LLMs Are a Dead End—Then Revealed Meta Fudged Their Benchmarks. Both Matter - Here's Why.

Every week I spend hours tracking what's actually happening in AI. Not the headlines, but the shifts underneath. There were over 200 stories I kept an eye on over the last week or so. And I think five of them really matter. I'm going to get to those five right away. Starting with the healthcare race. So OpenAI launched two products. They launched ChatGPT Health for consumers, which was very widely covered, where you can sync your health data, can ask questions about your body. And they also launched OpenAI for healthcare for enterprises, which is a HIPAA-compliant API. It has hospital system scale. Anthropic followed up 5 days later with Claude for healthcare, which features connectors to CMS databases and insurance claim systems. Obviously, the larger surface story is AI comes to medicine. Certainly part of the consumer story is that we can see from chat volume that consumers talk about health matters with LLMs regularly. And so to some extent the easiest read of this is that this is a defensive play by both ChatGPT and Claude because if you are talking with consumers about personal health matters you have a higher standard of care you have to exercise and it probably justifies the product investment on that basis alone. But I think there's another story here. The graveyard of healthcare AI is vast and well populated and it is tempting to see these skeptically as a result. I'm going to give you an example. IBM Watson did an oncology product a few years ago. It was supposed to revolutionize cancer care. It had some great initial results. It got sold for parts in 2022. Google's DeepMind has done a great job with protein folding and we have various drugs inspired by AI in the drug pipeline but to be honest none has yet hit the market and become a hit has none has yet reached millions of people so the natural question to ask when AI launches a healthcare product is what's different now here's the story I think nobody is talking about both companies are approaching the public markets and they need a story that is a little bit more than just a chatbot for work. Healthcare provides a really compelling IPO story. And you might think this is really early. Neither company has announced an IPO date. Why would we care about IPO at this point? I would argue back if you want a thriving healthcare story that can be part of your public company narrative, you got to start early. You got to start years before. You have existing partnerships with hospitals. You have health care data that's working. You could talk about millions of consumers benefiting from using your product for health care. That narrative takes time to develop. It is time to start now. And where a doctor submits paperwork to an insurance company for treatment approval. That's a $30 billion annual administrative burden. That is a real business. So I think many things can be true at once. The healthcare story can be both a story about these major model makers responding to real consumer existing demand for healthcare advice from AI and also a story about positioning themselves into a growing spend category in the economy that they can play on as they start to court public investors, eventually maybe late 26 maybe late 27 we don't have an idea yet when those IPO dates will be the larger question if we're thinking strategically is what happens to the healthcare AI market structure if both major foundation model companies are aggressively entering it.
