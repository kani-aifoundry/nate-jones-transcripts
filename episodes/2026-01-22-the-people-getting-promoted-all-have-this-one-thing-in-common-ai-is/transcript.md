# The People Getting Promoted All Have This One Thing in Common (AI Is Supercharging this Mindset)

Entry-level hiring at major tech companies has collapsed by more than 50% since 2019. Entry-level job postings across the US economy have declined by 29 percentage points between January 2024 and January 2026. The unemployment rate for recent college grads now exceeds the broader unemployment rate. So the traditional career ladder, the one where you could work at a brand name company, a brand name tech company, do your time, deliver competent results, climb from IC, individual contributor to a manager to a director to a VP. That ladder is being disassembled while people are still standing on. This is not a temporary hiring freeze. It is a fundamental restructuring of how careers work. The routine tasks that once trained newcomers, summarizing meetings, cleaning data, drafting memos, processing information, are precisely the tasks that generative AI handles with increasing competence. So, entry-level jobs today require experience that entry-level roles no longer supply. AI has cannibalized a lot of the low-risk work that used to teach people how to operate in complex organizations. And without those task rungs, the climb up the opportunity ladder has become steeper and for many impossible by traditional means. So what do you do when the ladder disappears? The answer is high agency. And I don't mean ordinary high agency. The kind that was always useful, always correlated with success. I mean off the charts paradigm-altering high agency. The distinction matters now in ways it never did before. Let me define what I mean because most people get this wrong. High agency is not a feeling. It is not a sense of empowerment or confidence. Interrogating your own emotions about whether you feel empowered leads you in circles and produces nothing useful. High agency is better understood through a concept that psychologist Julian Rotter identified in the 1950s. Locus of control. Here's a simple exercise that reveals where you actually stand. Take a piece of paper. Draw a circle on that piece of paper. Write down all the major elements in your life that matter to you. Family, friends, business, projects, education, your next promotion, the goals you've set. Whatever falls inside the circle is what you perceive is under your control. Whatever falls outside the circle is what you perceive is beyond your influence. When people with lower agency do this exercise, more of the significant elements in their lives end up outside that circle. The next promotion outside that's up to the manager. The degree of learning required to get there outside. I don't have the time. Major life changes outside the economy, the job market, circumstances beyond my control. For people with genuinely high agency, everything is inside the circle. Everything. The next promotion, the comp, the learning curve, the location, the skill development, absolutely everything without exception. Family goals, friendship goals, career goals, professional goals, AI goals, all inside. If your reaction to this is, "That's impossible, Nate. Some things are genuinely outside my control," then you have identified the precise mindset that distinguishes people who are high agency from those who are not. When high agency people hear that voice inside their head suggesting something is beyond their control, they respond with four words. That's a skill issue. It's a skill issue. I just don't know how to do it yet, but I can learn. Maybe I don't know how to get that promotion right now, but I can figure out what's missing. I can figure out who I need to talk to. I can figure out what value I need to create that I'm not creating currently. Low agency is the default setting of the human mind because it's safer. If things are outside your control, you're not responsible for them failing. But in the AI economy, safety is a hallucination. The only real security is the ability to solve problems that don't have instructions. And that starts with the radical assumption that every barrier you face is just a skill you haven't mastered yet. This isn't just self-help advice; it's a competitive necessity because AI is the ultimate low-agency tool. It can execute perfectly on clear instructions, but it can't decide what's worth doing or persist through the ambiguity of a truly novel challenge. That's where you come in. By moving everything into your circle of control, you're not just taking responsibility; you're staking your claim on the only value that can't be automated: the ability to act when the path is unclear.
