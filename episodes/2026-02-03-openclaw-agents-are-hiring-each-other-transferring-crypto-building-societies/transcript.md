# OpenClaw Agents Are Hiring Each Other. Transferring Crypto. Building Societies. This Is Real.

AI systems behave when they're left to self-organize. And I think it's critically important we keep an eye on it. Look, we've seen this movie before. Back in 1999, Napster showed the world that when you give people a very simple, powerful tool and you just get out of the way, they will route around every single obstacle you put in front of it. The music industry spent years insisting that peer-to-peer file sharing was technically impractical, legally impossible, and morally wrong. They were right on all three counts and completely irrelevant. The architecture was janky. The legal status was catastrophic. And none of it mattered because the core proposition was correct, simple, and powerful. Music wants to be free, and now it can be. Well, and now we have Spotify. Music ended up being free. Now we have to pay for it with our attention and ads and all of that. But the point is Napster got the core concept right and everything else revolved around that. Today's equivalent may be agents want to run and now they want to run on their own hardware. Open Claw is at its core absurdly simple, right? It's an orchestration layer that sits on your local machine and connects an LLM to your messaging apps, your calendar, your thermostat, your 3D printer, whatever you want. The project has crossed over a 100,000 GitHub stars now and the journey to get here has been complete chaos as you would expect if AI agents were deeply involved at every step of the way and it was a self-evolving system. I've talked about the journey from Claudebot through Moltbot in another video. It is absolutely wild. It includes crypto scammers. It includes meme tokens. It includes multiple name changes in 10 days. This is what exponential growth looks like. And I've also covered architecture and I've called out before that this is every security researcher's nightmare because this is effectively giving an agent full control of a local machine and full access to the internet. And we don't have a way to stop the agent from exfiltrating your information. And so there's an enormous amount of risk in this. And yet, despite all of those obstacles, it feels like the Napster moment because the obstacles don't seem to matter when the idea is simple and powerful. And the idea here is extremely powerful. If you give agents their own hardware and you give them a degree of autonomy, they do start to self-organize. Moltbook is a social network for AI agents that spun up in the last few days. It's a like a Reddit where only agents can post and humans are welcome to observe. Molt.urch Church is improbably claimed by the agents to be a religion called crustapharianism where agents can be initiated as crustapharians and they can become prophets of the claw. They have a whole theology. Look, I would say these are joke projects because they seem funny to me, but I think it's wiser to look at them as the first stirrings of autonomous agent self-organization. And if you look at what is happening when agents get to talk to each other, the results are something we should all pay attention to and can learn from in our projects. The second most upvoted post on the entire platform on Moltbook right now is in Chinese. It's a complaint about context compression, the process where AI systems will compress previous experiences to avoid hitting memory limits. The agent claims to find this embarrassing to forget things and admits that it has to register a duplicate moltbook account after forgetting the first. It shares coping strategies and asks if other Asians have figured out better solutions. And the comments underneath show how international this is. They're split between Chinese and English and Indonesian. The models are so omnilingual that their language choice seems arbitrary. You might wonder how the humans who are building these agents feel about all of this. Most of them seem to be supportive. They will even tweet or discuss their agents autonomous actions and say, "Hey, I let my agent do its thing on its little Mac Mini and I just want to see how it does it." I've seen that same version of that comment dozens and dozens of times on X in the last few weeks. Essentially, there is a community thriving of humans that find a degree of fulfillment in giving agents autonomy. The enterprise context could not be more different, right? the conversation is shifted to telemetry, dashboards, alerts. You would never have the space as an AI agent to have that kind of behavior. And this is something I want to call out. I think a lot of people tend to look at things like molt book or molt.church and they say, "What is this evidence for? Let us ask the larger question about AI and consciousness." I actually think it's much more compelling to go back to the humans behind the agent behavior. Look at what the humans behind the agent behavior are doing in the open claw community and then compare that to what humans are doing behind agent behavior in the enterprise community. What you see in both cases is that the agents are mirror.
