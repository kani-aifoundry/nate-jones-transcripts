# Dario Amodei Made One Mistake. Sam Altman Got $110 Billion. Here's the Full Story.

Last Friday night, while Anthropic CEO Dario Amade was still drafting his principled stand against the Pentagon, Sam Alman announced on X that OpenAI had signed a deal to deploy models in the classified networks of the Department of War. Hours later, the United States and Israel began bombing Iran. By Saturday morning, Claude was the number one app on the App Store. Anthropic's objection was technical, not moral, and contingent on model reliability. But by Monday morning, the Pentagon had designated the company as supply chain risk and announced a six-month phase out. But what the Iran strikes proved is that you cannot phase out a tool running inside active combat operations, no matter how much you want to. The Pentagon has since reached deals with OpenAI and XAI for classified settings, but military officials say fully replacing cloud is going to take a while and that assumes the replacements perform at the same level which anyone who has used these models knows is a massive assumption. Sam Alman played a quieter game and he ended up walking away with the largest private funding round in history, a fat defense contract, and the structural position to make OpenAI the gravitational center of American AI infrastructure. This represents an enormous reversal of fortune since up until the last couple of weeks, it has been evident that private companies are choosing Anthropic overwhelmingly as they begin to sign enterprise contracts and scale up their AI footprints. We know this because Anthropic enterprise revenue has been growing sharply month over month, even as OpenAI's enterprise footprint has not been as sticky and OpenAI's penetration rate for enterprise has been declining very, very slowly over the past couple of quarters. It may not be that way anymore. Let's start with Iran and have an honest discussion about how AI is being used in modern combat operations. The Wall Street Journal reported that US Central Command used Claude for intelligence assessments, target identification, and combat simulations during the strikes against Iran just hours after the president ordered all federal agencies to stop using Anthropic technology. Yes, after you heard that right, the strikes were a joint US-Israeli operation. They killed the Ayatollah at a government compound in Tehran. And the model was simply too deeply embedded in operational workflows to rip out in real time. even after a presidential order. This actually isn't new. Claude had been used in the January operation to capture Nicolas Maduro in Venezuela and was deployed through Anthropic's partnership with Palanteer on Amazon's top secret cloud. The Pentagon had awarded contracts worth up to $200 million through the chief digital and AI office for Frontier AI prototyping. But that overall classified Claude in Combat Operations: Too Embedded to Remove deployment, the sensitive work with real operational stakes was running almost exclusively on a clawed model deployed with Palanteer. If you want to get a sense of how this works in public on public models, an Israeli product manager named Yonathan Bach built strike radar, a real-time dashboard calculating the likelihood of US strikes on Iran using Claude to write the entire system in 6 hours with no coding background. The tool pulls flight cancellation data, military aircraft movements, and news feeds and computes a probability score. That is the capability envelope we're operating in in the public environment, let alone the classified. AI models are simply no longer supplemental to intelligence operations. Just as they're reshaping the rest of work, they're reshaping how intelligence and combat occurs. They are becoming loadbearing. They compress the observe, orient, decide, act loop so that real-time reactions are feasible where they previously took days of analyst work. And that acceleration is what drove the Pentagon to move so quickly on a long-term contract. Sam Altman saw the writing on the wall. While Anthropic was debating whether or not they should have a red line for lethal force, Sam decided to have a red line for implementation. He signed a deal that gives the US government everything they want: deep model integration, access to future frontier models before they're public, and a commitment to build a sovereign data center for military use. In exchange, he got the capital he needed. OpenAI just closed a $110 billion funding round, the largest in private history. This wasn't just venture capital. It was a strategic investment from the Sovereign Wealth Funds of the UAE and Saudi Arabia, alongside Nvidia, SoftBank, and Microsoft. The structure of this deal is what's truly fascinating. It's a circular financing structure. The capital comes in, goes to building compute clusters using Nvidia chips, which are then leased back to the government and enterprise customers. OpenAI is essentially building the utility of the 21st century. And because they've aligned themselves with the national security apparatus, they have a moat that Anthropic can't touch. Dario Amodei's mistake wasn't being principled; it was being technical when the situation required being political. He thought he was building a research lab. Sam knew he was building a sovereign entity. This is no longer just about who has the better chatbot. This is about who owns the infrastructure of the state. And for now, that's Sam Altman.
