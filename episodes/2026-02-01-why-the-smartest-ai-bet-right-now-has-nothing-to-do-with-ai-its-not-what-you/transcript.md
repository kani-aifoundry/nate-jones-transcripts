# Why the Smartest AI Bet Right Now Has Nothing to Do With AI (It's Not What You Think)

A week or so ago in Davos, Switzerland, Elon Musk told the World Economic Forum that we're approaching quote abundance for all. Ubiquitous AI, ubiquitous robotics, everything's going to be great. An explosion in the global economy, quote, truly beyond all precedent. He recommended we not save for retirement. Meanwhile, Dario Amode predicted half of white collar jobs would disappear. But apparently that's good because the abundance is just going to be everywhere. Look, the abundance narrative was everywhere at Davos. Echoed through every panel, every fireside chat, every opra ski conversation. But I want to suggest to you that the abundance economy is probably the wrong frame for most of us to think about the next few years. And I want to suggest that we think about the scarcity economy instead. Because if you are trying to find where value actually concentrates, if you're trying to find where money is made, you don't look for abundance. You look for bottlenecks. And right now AI is creating some of the biggest, most expensive bottlenecks we've ever seen. And that is exactly where the smart money is going. Not into the AI itself, but into the bottlenecks that AI is creating. So today I want to talk about the four major bottlenecks that are going to define the next five years of the AI transition. And why if you understand these bottlenecks, you can find ways to succeed in the AI economy. So, let's talk about bottlenecks. Cognizant released telling research on AI claiming that it could unlock could is the keyword and a half trillion dollars in US labor productivity. But there was a massive caveat that no one paid attention to from that research. The value will only materialize if quote businesses can implement it effectively. That is the biggest asterisk I've ever seen. And most businesses according to the CEO of Cognizant, Robbie Kumar, have not yet done the hard work. I think that's very true. There it is. That's the gap between the abundance narrative that sounds so good in Switzerland and the reality. It's not about capability of models. It's about implementation. It's about value capture. The AI already exists, but the trillion dollar value that people like to talk about doesn't exist yet because the bottlenecks are in the way. And the bottleneck is where the leverage is. If you've ever read The Goal by Eliyahu Goldratt, which is the Bible of operations and systems thinking, you know that in any system there is only ever one bottleneck at a time. One part of the process that limits the output of the whole thing. If you improve anything else in the system except for the bottleneck, you've accomplished nothing because you didn't improve the bottleneck. But if you improve the bottleneck just a little bit, everything will move. Look, this is basic systems thinking and it's also something that most people ignore. They optimize for whatever is visible, whatever is comfortable, whatever they're already good at. They work harder instead of differently. They add capacity where there's already lots of capacity in the system and they ignore the one thing that's actually holding everything back. This is why the first industrial revolution didn't happen in 1750 when the steam engine was invented. It happened much later when we figured out how to use the steam engine to solve the energy bottleneck in factories and the transport bottleneck with railroads. Every dominant organizational form emerged to dissolve a specific bottleneck. The Dutch East India companies solved the capital lockup problem of multi-year oceanic voyages. Railroads cracked the energy constraint on overland transport. Banks emerged to allocate capital across time. Stock exchanges aggregated capital at scales that exceeded any private fortune. Walmart solved the information bottleneck in retail supply chains just by knowing what was selling where and getting it there before stockouts. The pattern is consistent. Whoever solves the binding constraints captures disproportionate value. Everybody else participates in the abundance that's created. The AI era absolutely has its own bottlenecks and they're not the ones most people are watching. First, the binding constraint on AI capability is increasingly atoms, not bits. Jensen Huang told Davos that AI needs more than just better algorithms. It needs more energy. It needs more cooling. It needs more copper. It needs more specialized construction. We are moving from a world where scaling was a software problem to a world where scaling is a physical infrastructure problem. The second bottleneck is trust. In a world of infinite synthetic content, the binding constraint is no longer the ability to create. It's the ability to verify. As the cost of creation drops to zero, the cost of verification goes to infinity. We are entering a trust deficit where the most valuable assets are the ones that can be proven to be real, human, and accurate. The third bottleneck is integration. This is the gap between what a model can do in a research lab and what it can do in a legacy enterprise workflow. The constraint isn't intelligence; it's the glue. It's the APIs, the data cleanup, the security permissions, and the change management. The trillion dollar productivity gains are locked behind millions of boring integration problems. And finally, the fourth bottleneck is individual judgment. As AI levels the playing field for technical skills, the binding constraint shifts to what we might call taste or problem-finding. When everyone has an expert assistant, the difference between success and failure is the ability to ask the right questions and the judgment to know which AI-generated answers are actually good. In this video, I want to dive deep into each of these four bottlenecks. I want to show you why they are the strategic lens you should be using to navigate the next few years. Because while everyone else is chasing the abundance, you can be the one who solves the scarcity. Scarcity lives. And we haven't been honest about that. The question isn't whether to believe in the coming abundance as an article of faith. No, no, no, no, no. The question is where are the bottlenecks and are you positioning yourself and your business to solve them? That's really the only question that matters and it doesn't get enough airtime.
