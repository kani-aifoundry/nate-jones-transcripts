This week, the two people most likely to build artificial general intelligence sat on the same stage in Switzerland and agreed it's coming faster than anyone outside the major labs realizes and half of entry-level white collar jobs could be at risk. The same week, Apple admitted it lost the foundation model race and XAI closed the largest funding expansion of XAI's Colossus supercomputers in Memphis, Tennessee. The company ended 2025 with over 1 million H100 GPU equivalents across Colossus 1 and 2. Grok 5 is currently in training there. XAI claims roughly 600 million monthly active users across X and Grok apps making it the largest consumer facing AI deployment outside of Google and OpenAI. The raise coincided with a safety crisis. Grok generate generated inappropriate deep fakes of real people including minors and triggered regulatory probes in the EU, UK, India, Malaysia and France. Despite all of this, XAI landed a Department of Defense deal and Grok is now the DoD's AI agents platform and also Grok powers prediction markets, Poly Market and Call. So why does this matter? Three labs now have clear runway to survive the multi-year scaling race. OpenAI, Anthropic, and XAI. You can pencil in Google over the top as the fourth because even though funding is not designated, Google is such a money machine, they can fund AI as a bet. Everyone besides those four is operating against shorter timelines and even OpenAI and Anthropic have more fundraising risk than the other two at this point. Partly because Elon has proved himself such an extraordinary fundraiser. The Grog safety failures are instructive in the context of the fund raise. XAI was able to close its largest round even while facing active investigations in five countries. We've also seen active investigations and lawsuits continue against OpenAI, but not really impact funding timelines. The way I read that is that investors have a very long-term perspective on the value of AI at this point, and they are willing to look past a lot of these initial building issues and assume that AI is going to start to clean up and mature as a product offering as the ball moves forward into the future. We will see if that's true. Story number two. At Davos, the economist moderated a session called the day after AGI featuring featuring Daario Amadei from Anthropic and Deise Hasabis from Google Deep Mind. Yes, they're competitors, but really the point was to have a conversation about what happens in a world with AGI. Overall, there was a lot of agreement between the two. Amadei is a little bit more aggressive in his belief in how quickly technology is progressing. He reaffirmed his prediction that AGI will emerge this year in 2026 or 2027 driven by an accelerating feedback loop by AI writing its own code. He revealed that Anthropic engineers rarely write code by hand anymore. AI does it and humans review. Uh and he was the one that warned that there is a significant risk of entry-level white collar workers becoming becoming replaceable as models reach human level in in some cognitive skills. He described this process as a compression of of human effort where models can essentially handle tasks that previously required multiple years of schooling. For builders and operators, this this part of the conversation was more specific than than usual in Davos. When Amadei talked about entry-level workers, he specifically meant people with about zero to three years of of domain experience. These are essentially humans who are following standard operating procedures in digital environments. As models begin to scale, he anticipates that more and more of that SOP following will move to agents rather than rather than entry-level humans. Uh on the flip side, we had Demis Hassabis and he pushed back on the timeline, but he agreed with the end state. He suggested that AGI is more likely by the end of this decade, but he did not frame it as as a replacement for humanity. He pointed out that every time we automate a task, we often create a need for a higher order task that needs to move one step ahead. So he sees more of a symbiotic path. However, both of them agreed that the the biggest short-term change is coming to coding. He described how Google is using models to write more than 25% of their production code. The takeaway here is that that the the leading voices in AI have converged on a single truth. Whether it's this year or five years from now, the entry-level digital labor market is facing a structural shift. If you are starting your career in AI, your focus should not be on learning a single skill like coding or writing SOPs, but on mastering the oversight of agents that do those things. Story number three. Apple. For the first time, Apple leadership officially admitted they missed the initial wave of high-end frontier models. In an internal town hall, Apple executives acknowledged that that while they have great on-device features with Apple Intelligence, they essentially depend on Google's Gemini models and cloud technology. I can't sugarcoat it. This is a big loss for OpenAI. OpenAI should have been in the running here and this is a big piece of revenue. The deal reportedly costs Apple a billion dollars a year. While the rest of the world was was moving and iterating in public, Apple was trying to build the perfect integrated product in secret and by the time they were ready to release, the frontier had shifted miles ahead of them. This deal effectively cements Google's position as the utility provider of high-end AI intelligence in the Apple ecosystem. It also explains why Apple is being so so collaborative with with other model providers and keeping the door open for for integrations.
